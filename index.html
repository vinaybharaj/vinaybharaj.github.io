Fortune 100 CPGNorth AmericaCost Transformation

Zero-based spend program cut $30M in annual indirect cost — and made it stick in the budget

$30M

100%

Closed-loop spend methodology, visibility to control

The situation

Indirect spend across the client's North American operations was growing unchecked. Ownership was fragmented across functions, spend data sat in multiple ERPs, and prior cost programs had delivered savings that quietly eroded back into budgets within two years.

My mandate

Run the zero-based spend diagnostic and build a savings pipeline that finance would actually adopt into annual budgets — not a slideware number.

What I actually did

  • Built the spend cube. Pulled, cleansed, and consolidated GL/AP data across ERPs; mapped every dollar to a common category taxonomy using AI-assisted classification plus manual validation with function and finance owners.
  • Benchmarked every category. Profiled spend per FTE and spend-as-%-of-revenue against proprietary peer benchmarks to locate the outliers worth attacking first.
  • Ran cross-functional ideation. Facilitated value-targeting workshops with category owners across demand, specification, and price levers — turning hypotheses into quantified, owner-signed initiatives.
  • Prioritized and sequenced. Scored the initiative pipeline on value vs. ease, built the execution wave plan, and stood up the executive tracking cadence with stage-gated savings status.
  • Made it stick. Established category ownership governance — a shared accountability model between procurement, the business, and finance — and integrated validated savings directly into annual budget targets to prevent erosion.

Leadership signal

Ran the face-off with client category owners and finance leadership through every validation session. The savings case was adopted into annual budgets — the strongest form of client sign-off a cost program can get.

The situation

A leading European pharma distributor was rolling out an ML-driven forecasting and replenishment platform (RELEX). Dozens of integration points — product, supplier, order, and sales flows — meant one bad feed could silently corrupt forecasts across the network. Data readiness was the single biggest program risk.

My mandate

Stand up and run the program's PMO and own data readiness end-to-end: if the platform went live on bad data, nothing else the program did would matter.

What I actually did

  • Stood up the PMO. Owned RAID management, steering-committee reporting, and coordination between the client, the platform vendor, and internal delivery teams.
  • Mapped every interface. Documented the standard integration set — master data, transactional flows, and reference data — and defined acceptance criteria per feed.
  • Built the validation engine. Designed and built an Alteryx-based engine running 250+ automated checks — completeness, referential integrity, business-rule conformance — across all interfaces in seconds instead of days of manual sampling.
  • Led the offshore team. Directed an 8-person team across data engineering and testing workstreams through go-live cutover cycles.
  • Turned it into an asset. Packaged the engine with documentation so subsequent implementations could reuse it from day one.

Leadership signal

Recognized by both firm and vendor leadership — the validation tool was adopted as a reusable accelerator on subsequent platform implementations.

The situation

Short shelf-life categories — bill-of-materials production items and hospitality lines — suffered waste and availability gaps at the same time. The ML engine was live, but custom configurations had created edge cases the planners no longer trusted.

My mandate

Own the demand planning and inventory workstreams end-to-end: make the machine's ordering decisions trustworthy enough that category teams would stop overriding them.

What I actually did

  • Diagnosed the edge cases. Traced availability failures back to specific custom configuration choices in forecasting and replenishment workflows — not to the underlying models.
  • Reconfigured the ML workflows. Tuned RELEX forecasting and replenishment settings for BOM-driven fresh production and hospitality demand patterns, where a day of error means the product is in the bin.
  • Built planner trust deliberately. Embedded forecast-variance tracking into the weekly category decision rhythm so planners could see why the system ordered what it ordered — and when to intervene.
  • Ran the workstream with client leadership. Owned the working relationship with category leadership through the change, from diagnosis to adopted process.

Leadership signal

End-to-end workstream ownership with client category leadership — the adoption problem, not just the configuration problem, was the deliverable.

The situation

Visibility was fragmented across demand, inventory, and logistics systems. Exceptions surfaced late, root-cause analysis took days, and every function watched its own dashboard while the network-level picture belonged to no one.

My mandate

Define what an intelligent execution control tower should be for this business — the vision, the KPI architecture, and a product roadmap the organization could actually build against.

What I actually did

  • Set the vision and scope. Framed the control tower as a decision product, not a dashboard: from see (visibility) to sense (alerting) to diagnose (root cause) to act (predictive and prescriptive resolution).
  • Designed the KPI architecture. Defined the metric tree connecting exception signals to business outcomes — service, inventory, and cost — so alerts ranked by dollar impact, not noise.
  • Built the now/next/later roadmap. Sequenced capabilities by data readiness and value, partnering with engineering and analytics teams on the near-real-time visibility layer.
  • Aligned the executives. Drove the design through supply chain leadership reviews until it was adopted as the baseline for the transformation program.

Leadership signal

Set the KPI and product agenda that supply chain leadership adopted as the program baseline — the definition of the transformation, not a contribution to it.

The situation

New liquor regulations threatened the vast majority of a flagship category's sales at a leading Southern-hemisphere premium retailer. At the same time, the business wanted a portfolio of new growth concepts matured from idea to investable roadmap — fast.

My mandate

Build the category defense and growth roadmap, and run the sizing and prioritization machinery across the wider portfolio of solution concepts.

What I actually did

  • Sized the prize rigorously. Built top-down market sizing per concept — addressable population, basket, and frequency logic — turning vision statements into rand-denominated opportunity ranges.
  • Prioritized with discipline. Ran concepts through an attractiveness-vs-feasibility framework to select which to unlock now versus incubate — focus over spread.
  • Built the de-risking plan. For the regulated category: national fulfilment unlock through DC-enabled home delivery, digital POS response in affected regions, and a membership-led engagement model to retain the customer base.
  • Sequenced now/next/later roadmaps. Mapped features and their enablement requirements — fulfilment infrastructure, technology, channels, customer insight — into executable 0–6, 6–12, and 12+ month waves.
  • Optimized the channel play. Led omni-channel strategy including store selection and online fulfilment optimization for the expansion concepts.

Leadership signal
